2. From Classical to Statistical Mechanics

2.1 Lagrangian formulation

Principle of Stationary Action

To derive the equation of motion in Lagrange formulation we will use the principle of stationary that states: the physical path that a given system will take between two points is the one for which the action S is an extremum (극점).

The action integral is

$$
S = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} L\left[\mathbf{q}(t), \dot{\mathbf{q}}(t)\right] , dt
$$

Mathematically condition for the extremum of S is $\delta S = 0$.

$$
\delta S = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} \sum_{\alpha=1}^{3N} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_\alpha} - \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_\alpha} \right) \right) \delta q_\alpha(t) , dt
$$

The first term in third line cancels due to the constraint $\delta q(t_0) = \delta q(t_1) = 0$.
Since variations $\delta q_\alpha(t)$ are independent and arbitrary the requirement $\delta S = 0$ can only be satisfied when the term in brackets vanishes independently for all $\alpha$.

This leads to the Euler-Lagrange equation.

$$
\frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_\alpha} \right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_\alpha} = 0 \quad (\alpha = 1, \dots, 3N)
$$

  • Equivalance with Newton’s second law

  • Conserved Quantities
    The equations of motion in the Lagrangian formulation are given by the Euler-Lagrance equation:
    $$
    \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_\alpha} \right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_\alpha} = 0 \quad (\alpha = 1, \dots, 3N)
    $$

2.2 Hamiltonian formulation

The Hamiltonian 𝐻 provides a more accurate description of a system’s energy changes when external forces are acting on it because it explicitly incorporates both momentum and position (which are affected by external forces) in its formulation. Unlike the total energy 𝐸, which can only describe the energy in terms of position and velocity, the Hamiltonian also captures the impact of external influences, making it a more complete and dynamic tool for understanding energy changes in systems with external forces.

The Hamiltonian fuction is..

$$
H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{3N} p_\alpha \dot{q}_\alpha - L(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}})
$$

Here is the equation written out:

  • $p_i$ is the generalized momentum
  • $q$ is the generalized coordinate
  • U(q) is the potential energy

$$
H({\mathbf{r}i}, {\mathbf{p}_i}) = \sum{i=1}^N \mathbf{p}i \cdot \mathbf{r}_i - L({\mathbf{r}_i}, {\dot{\mathbf{r}}_i})
= \sum{i=1}^N mi \dot{\mathbf{r}}_i^2 - \frac{1}{2} \sum{i=1}^N mi \dot{\mathbf{r}}_i^2 + U({\mathbf{r}_i})
= \frac{1}{2} \sum{i=1}^N mi \dot{\mathbf{r}}_i^2 + U({\mathbf{r}_i})
= \sum{i=1}^N \frac{\mathbf{p}_i^2}{2 m_i} + U({\mathbf{r}_i})
$$

Hamiltonian, therefore, corresponds to the total energy E of the system.
H = K + U = E

This property constrains are phase space vector to a (6N - 1)-dimensional constrant-energy hypersurface.

    The Legendre transform is defined as
    $$F(y) = yx(y) - f[x(y)]$$.

    $$

    To obtain the Hamiltonian from the Lagrangian we do a change of variables so that the Hamiltonian is a function of generalized coordinates and conjugate(활용) momentum.
    $$L(q, \dot{q}) \to H(q, p)$$

    $$dH = \sum_{a=1}^{3N} \left( \frac{\partial H}{\partial q_a} dq_a + \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_a} dp_a \right)$$

    $$dH = \sum_{a=1}^{3N} \left( d(p_a \dot{q}_a) - dL \right)$$

2.3 Ensemble Concept

To reduce the high dimensionalty of the problem, we introduce a statistical description of the problem via ensemble concept.

An ensemble could be a collection of M systems each containing N particles and evolving under the same microscopic interactions but starting from different initial conditions.

  • Ensemble Distribution Fuction

The microstates of an ensemble do not occupy the total phase space because they correspond to a particular macroscopic set of observables (관측 값 집합에 해당하는 부분만 차지).
$\rho(Z, t)$ describes the likelihood of finding a system at time t in state Z. It is a probability distribution fuction. (시간 t에서 상태 Z에 있을 확률 밀도)

$$
\rho(Z, t) \geq 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \int_{\Gamma} \rho(Z, t)dZ = 1
$$

Hypervolume

$$
dZ = \prod_{a=1}^{3N} dq_a dp_a
$$

  • Ensemble Average
    A macroscopic observable of the ensemble A is calculated by performing an average over the M systems in the ensemble:

    $$
    A = \frac{1}{M} \sum*{\lambda=1}^M a(Z*\lambda) \equiv \langle a \rangle
    $$

    $$
    A = \langle a \rangle = \int_{\Gamma} a(Z) \rho(Z, t)dZ. \tag
    $$

    A is an expectation value with respect to the phase space probability distribution function $\rho(Z, t)$.

1. Classical Mechanics

1.1 Microscopic Laws of Motion

Phase space and Trajectory

$$F_i(\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N) = m_i \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_i \quad \forall i = 1, \ldots, N$$
$F_i$ is the force acting on particle i due to all other particles in the system.
It’s a force-centric description.

1.2 Lagrangian formulation

The lagrangian L is defined in terms of kinetic K and potential energy U as
$$L(\mathbf{r}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N, \dot{\mathbf{r}}_1, \ldots, \dot{\mathbf{r}}_N) = K(\dot{\mathbf{r}}_1, \ldots, \dot{\mathbf{r}}_N) - U(\mathbf{r}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N)$$
It’s a energy centric description

  • Kinetic Energy
    The kinetic energy of a single particle is defined as a work needed to accelerate the particle from rest velocity.
    For the N-particle system, the kinetic energy is thereore given by
    $$K = \sum_{i=1}^N \frac{1}{2} m_i \dot{\mathbf{r}}_i^2$$

  • Potential Energy and Conservative Forces
    A conservative force is a force that can be derived from a scalar function called potential energy
    $$\mathbf{F}_i(\mathbf{r}_1, \dots, \mathbf{r}_N) = - \frac{\partial U(\mathbf{r}_1, \dots, \mathbf{r}_N)}{\partial \mathbf{r}_i}$$

    • The negative sign indicates that the force acts in the direction that decreases the potential energy. This is a reflection of how systems tend to move towards configurations that minimize their potential energy.

  • Generalized Coordinates
    A cartesian coordinates ${r_1, \dots, r_N}$ to generalized coordinates ${q_1, \dots, q_3N}$, where we assume that there is a unique coordinate transformation from one the other.
    Also for a system with N unconstrained particles, we have 3N configurational degrees of freedom. However for $N_c$ holonomic constraints,
    $$f_j(q_1, \dots, q_{3N}, t) = 0 \quad \text{for} \quad j = 1, \dots, N_c$$
    Then the system has $3N - N_c$ configurational degrees of freedom.

    In generalized coordinates, the Lagrangian reads
    $$L(q, \dot{q}) = K(q, \dot{q}) - U(q)$$
    ex) We can perform a coordinate transformation form r = (x, y) to the generalized coordinates $q = {r, \theta}$ thus, $y = - r \cos \theta$

4. Impurities in Solids

The addition of impurity atoms to a metal results in the formation of solid solution and/or a new second phase, depending on the kinds of impurity, their concentrations, and the temperature of the alloy. The present discussion is concerned with the notion of a solid solution.
Regard to alloys, solute 용질 and solvent 용매 are terms that are commonly employed. Solvent is the element or compound that is present in the greatest amount; on occasion, solvent atoms are also called host atoms.

Solid Solutions
impurity point defects are found in solid solutions, of which there are two types: substitutional and interstitial.

For the substitutional type, solute or impurity atoms replace or substitute for the host atoms. Several fetures of the solute and solvent atoms determine the degree to which the former dissolves in the latter.

These are expressed as four Hume-Rothery rules.

  1. Atomic size factor
    Appreciable(상당한) quantities of a solute may be accommodated in this type of solid solution only when the difference in atomic radii between the two atom types is less than about +-15%.
  2. Crystal structure
    The crystal structures for metals of both atom types must be the same.
  3. Electronegativity factor
    The more electropositive one element and the more electronegative the other, the greater the likelihood that they will form an intermatallic compound instead of a substitutional solid solution. So the smaller the difference in electronegativity, the more likely they are to form a solid solution.
  4. Valences
    Other factors being equal, a metal has more of a tendency to dissolve another metal of higher valency than to dissolve one of a lower valency.

An example of substitutional solid solution is found for copper and nickel. With regard to the aforementioned rules that govern degree of solubility, the atomic radii for copper and nickel are 0.128 and 0.125nm, respectively. Both have the FCC crystal structure.
